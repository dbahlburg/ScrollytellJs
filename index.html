This is a story about the life of
Antarctic krill

By Dominik Bahlburg
21.02.2021

Antarctic krill or Euphausia superba is a small crustacean of enormous importance to the ecosystem of the Southern Ocean. Even though the word krill is often associated with Antarctic krill which only occurs in Antarctica, there are a total of 31 different krill species which inhabit the oceans worldwide. However, none of the other 30 species comes close to Antarctic krill in terms of abundance and importance to the regional ecosystem. With a total estimated biomass of up to 379 Million tonnes, Antarctic krill not only dominates the Southern Ocean ecosystem but is also considered one of the biomass-richest species on earth with its closest competitors being cattle and humans.
Due to this enormous abundance, Antarctic krill is an important prey item for many Southern Ocean predators such as crabeater seals, multiple species of penguins or even humpback, blue and fin whales. These close linkages between the crustaceans and its pursuers makes it an influential species in numerous ways. In a study from 2007, Dr. Jefferson Hinke (NOAA) and colleagues showed that annual fluctuations in krill abundance can be a main driver of penguin breeding success around the South Shetland islands which are one of the krill hotspots in the Southern Ocean.

In another example, researchers continue to explore the “krill-surplus-hypothesis” which aims to explain increased penguins abundances during the 1970s and decreasing populations sizes from there on. According to the hypothesis, the strong reduction in Antarctic whale and seal populations caused by commercial harvesting until the 1970s reduced the feeding pressure of those predators on regional krill populations. This food surplus enabled penguins, which in contrast were not commercially harvested, to grow in population size during these times. With the ongoing recovery of whale and seal populations, competition for krill among its predators increases and penguin populations start to drop.
Both examples demonstrate how krill can directly and indirectly drive population dynamics of other species in a complex network of trophic relationships.

Development of Krill

Birth
The life of Antarctic krill starts as an egg of a diameter of 0.6mm. Usually, females spawn in deep waters where the depth exceeds 1000m. After release, the eggs sink down the water column at a rate of around 100-200m per day. Scientists believe that krill prefer these deep water spawning grounds in order to avoid eggs reaching the sea floor before hatching occurs. During this descent, the embryo undergoes multiple cell divisions until it develops into a nauplius larva and hatches after approximately 6 days.

Nauplius I and II
The nauplius stage is a developmental stage shared by most marine crustaceans. Therefore, it is not unique to Antarctic krill but can also be found in the life cycles of lobsters, barnacles or copepods. As the first free-swimming stage, the nauplii of Antarctic krill start ascending in the water column until they reach depths of approximately 200m. The nauplius stage consists of the nauplius I and II substages. Nauplii are characterized by their oval body shape, their appendages and a rudimentary eye. It takes the krill around 9 days to develop through the nauplius stages until the metanauplius stage is reached.

Metanauplius
During the metanauplius stage, krill starts developing its carapace. The carapace is the “main shield” of the exoskeleton, an armour-like shell which surrounds the small crustaceans. Krill grow by molting which means that the exoskeleton is regularly casted off and replaced by a new one. This also enables individuals to make sudden morphological leaps during larval development.

Calyptotis I
After 13 days, krill reaches the calyptotis stage. During the calyptotis stage, most notably the abdomen starts to elongate which leads to the characteristic long body shape which krill keep for the rest of their lives. In addition, the stalked compound eyes are clearly visible now. This is also the first stage where krill actively feeds since the mouth parts are now formed and functioning.

Calyptotis III
In the third and final calyptotis stage, the abdomen is clearly segmented and the compound eyes well developed. At this stage, the body is also partially covered by red pigments which will later be responsible for the red colour of adult krill. The star-shaped pigments are retractable and depending on their expansion, the colour of individuals can change from almost translucent to intense red. At this point of their development, the krill have reached a size of around 3mm.

Furcilia
Furcilia larvae already strongly resemble juvenile krill but are much smaller with a maximum size of 11mm in their sixth and final substage. While going through the furcilia stage, the eyes start to extend out of the carapace, the legs and mouth parts fully developed and most importantly, the feeding basket is now present. The feeding basket is formed by a number of front legs with very fine appendages. Through rhythmic movements of those legs, krill filter the surrounding water with astonishing efficiency. They are able to retain almost 100% of particles down to sizes as low as 1 μm.

Adult
2 years after hatching, krill finally reach the adult stage. As adults they grow to a maximum size of 60mm. In contrast to the juvenile stage which comes in between furcilia and adults, the reproduction organs are now differentiated and functional. Krill usually start reproducing in their second year of life and do so annually until they reach their expected life span of 6 years.

The Southern Ocean

The Southern Ocean
The Southern Ocean surrounds the Antarctic continent and is composed of sectors of the Atlantic, Indian and Pacific Ocean. It is an Ocean with extreme seasonality which makes it an especially challenging home for its inhabitants.

Thermal isolation
The Southern Ocean is different to other oceans in that it is very isolated. Its water masses are typically cold, in some areas less than -2°C. The extend of the Southern Ocean is broadly defined by the Southern Polar Convergence which describes the edge area where cold Antarctic water masses meet warmer Subantarctic waters. The result is distinctive sharp latitudinal temperature gradient.

The Antarctic Circumpolar Current
One of the most important contributing factors driving the isolation of the Southern Ocean is the Antarctic Circumpolar Current (ACC). The ACC is a wind-driven current and one of the strongest on earth with flow rates of up to 4 km/h (or 35'000 km per year). It constantly transports water around Antarctica in a clockwise direction and prevents the mixing of Antarctic and more northerly surface waters.

The ACC not only contributes to the separation of water masses but also acts as a "highway" around Antarctica. Particles trapped in the ACC can travel as far as 17'000 kilometers in only three years as shown in the map. Adult krill are usually not considered as passive drifters but researchers discuss the role of the ACC in connecting different krill populations across the Southern Ocean.
